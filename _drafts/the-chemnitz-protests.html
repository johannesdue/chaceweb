On Monday evening, a demonstration

So, what happened in Chemnitz?

Sunday night three German men are stabbed during a city festival, one of them dies. Two suspects arrested, of Syrian and Iraqi background. Circumstances unclear, police denying rumors about victims having tried to stop attackers from sexually harassing a woman. On Monday, AfD and local football ultras mobilize. At 3, AfD rally gathers 100 people and proceeds peacefully. Then, at 4, police decide to close down the ongoing city festival. The second rally, announced by "Kaotic Chemnitz" football ultras, begins at 4:30, gathering 800 people. Lots of festival-goers join in. Police unable to control the crowd, which breaks through the lines and marches on. Crowd grows to about 6000. Some 1500 counter-protesters show up. Police try to keep them separate, but at about 9 the situation deteriorates. Bottles and fireworks fly through the air, in some instances foreign-looking people are chased and attacked.

A couple of thoughts:

1) Was it a "neo-Nazi mob"? Of course, there were violent Nazis there, and journalists love to focus on those (and they should). But many pictures, videos, and eyewitness accounts suggest lots of protesters were peaceful, "ordinary people". Even though militants use the crowd as a platform and cover for engaging in violence, it doesn't mean everyone in the crowd supports them.

2) Don't dismiss legitimate concerns about immigration and crime as simply racism. While Chemnitz isn't particularly high-crime, local police stats suggest 1 in 4 crimes committed by foreigners/asylum seekers. Country-wide, police data suggest over-representation of asylum seekers on certain particularly serious (and far-right-mobilizing) types of violent crime. People with authoritarian predispositions will get fired up over this everywhere.

3) Violence went both ways. DW: "As many as 18 protesters from both sides and two police officers were injured. Four far-right protesters were attacked and injured on their way home by a group of 15-20 people; two were hospitalized, police say."

4) Chemnitz is in East Germany, which carries a particular historical legacy of right-wing violence, which I wrote about here.
